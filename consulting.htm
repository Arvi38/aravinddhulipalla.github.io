US Department of Transportation/Transportation System Center :

From November 1997 to March 2005 guided the Volpe’s strategic planning and analysis team on national and international transportation cutting edge technologies including their priorities and R&D expenditures. During this period detailed analysis on unifying the First Responders on varying communication facets by disparate teams. During this period a detailed analysis of R&D expenditures in transportation sector and its correlation with the progress a country makes was presented to RSPA, renamed Research Innovative Technology Administration (RITA) in 2005. In addition in order to address the vulnerability of our national transportation system in the aftermath of 9/11, a number of scenario were developed and presented to Washington DC in the form of white papers as stated under “Other Research or Creative Activities” on page 10.

From November 1995 to October 1997 guided the Volpe Center’s special project’s team and FAA’s safety division on radiation hazards, ionizing as well as non-ionizing, especially calculating the safe distances under a variety of scenarios of EM non-ionizing radiations and ionizing radiations and helping the team in preparing the latest safety manual and order. Guided client/server integration team, on how to transfer data voice and video intelligently from client to client without burdening the server unduly, (November, 1993 - October 1995). From November 1993-October 1993 technically advised the team of Intelligent Vehicle Highway System (IVHS) in general, and to Vehicle Collision Avoidance System (VCAS) in particular, especially on development of the suitable imaging algorithms to be employed in VCAS. From November 1988–October 1991 technically advised the team of Safety and Security Systems group on developing the universal standard in comparing the chemical and electronic photographs and their transmission employing the latest pattern recognition and image enhancement techniques.

From July 1986 to October 1988 technically advised the team of Advanced Automation and Logistics Systems (AALS) group on designing the Local Area Network "Extended LAN" for the code-50 integrating IBM P. C.'s, Apollo's, and VAX 785 as core hardware and Novell's 286 Netware as main software with the thin Ethernet cable as prime medium. Since January 1985 till June 1986, the specifications for Maintenance Control Center were developed in accordance with the criterion laid down by the FAA. In addition, the acquisition, testing, and diagnostics procedures were developed in their entirety for acquiring the new systems in order to modernize the FAA's control towers. A paper entitled "Design of Maintenance Control Center Based on Data Requirements" was prepared and presented as listed under Research Activities.

From January 1982 to December 1984, worked as a Faculty Fellow at the computer center of DOT/TSC, principal design engineer for developing the interface between ADAGE graphic terminal and PRIME's 550 CPU; responsible for the entire hardware and software development for the project. Responsibilities also included performing feasibility studies, trade off studies, long range planning, writing acquisition plans and defining operations, maintenance and programming requirements for the Real Time Computer Facility and mini/micro computer systems. During the Summer of 1979 and from May 1980 to December 1981 employed as a Faculty Fellow in the Telecommunications Branch on the Beacon Collision Avoidance System, (BCAS). Conducted the feasibility studies of BCAS, its compatibility with the existing surveillance systems like Automatic Traffic Control Radar Beacon Systems (ATCRBS), Automatic Traffic Advisory & Resolution Services (ATARS) and Air Traffic Control (ATC). The BCAS capability of providing safe-separation while maintaining an acceptable false alarm rate lesser to ATARS and conflict alert. Mode Singularities and Garble & Fruits in high traffic density environment and constancy of antenna rotation were also investigated. Mathematical modeling and computer simulation for the SSR antenna under non-ideal situation was carried out in its entirely.

The technical expertise on passive, semi-active/active and Full-BCAS was developed through an intensive study of the literature available on the subject and participate with FAA and TSC project staff. Geometrical analysis of the three-dimensional two-site BCAS problem was carried out in its entirety. Technically advised group in carrying out (i) covariance analysis of three dimensional equations (ii) the simulation of the covariance analysis (iii) the feasibility study of estimating the target's altitude, when the target is equipped only with Mode A transponder (iv) the determination of range and altitude with the geometry of two target aircraft equipped with BCAS without ground surveillance (v) the simulation of the range and altitude in the situation in order to avoid collisions absolutely (vi) carried out System Modeling for secondary surveillance radar (SSR)'s antenna under non ideal situation of nonuniform rotation rate and its impact on calculation of intruder aircraft's range and bearing, and (vii) carried out error analysis of threat alert and collision avoidance systems (TCAS) making use of optimal estimation techniques including Kalman filtering that provides an upper bound on the system errors by indicating lower bound on the system errors for a specific set of selected scenarios. The reports and papers are included in the resume under publications.

Digital Equipment Corporation :

Summer 1977. Worked for Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC), Maynard, Massachusetts in Power Supply Diagnostics Group. Technically advised the team in developing and designing the automation systems for diagnosing the switching power supplies. The automatic power supply tester was a unique development embracing the latest innovative firmware, and depicted the list of components in probable-failure order in the event of fault diagnosis in order to facilitate the maintenance staff. The paper and report entitled "Computer Diagnostics for Power Supplies" was prepared and presented as listed under publications.

MIT/Lincoln Laboratory :

Summer 1976. Worked for Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), Lincoln Laboratory, Lexington, Massachusetts. Developed a High Speed Punch (HSP) and a High Speed Reader (HSR) interface with Intersil microprocessor to be used by Aircraft Simulation Techniques group. The reader interface of I-6100 with Econored 150 was successfully designed and the punch interface with Epson 6110 was also successfully designed. These advanced special purpose laboratory models were tested and built, and deployed in simulation studies. A paper and report entitled, "Interfacing of Mini/Microcomputers to Peripherals" was prepared as listed under publications.

Digital Equipment Corporation :

Summer 1975. Worked for Digital Equipment Corporation, Maynard, Massachusetts in Information Processing Center (IPC). Technically advised the team in designing advanced circuits using PDP-8 for custom-designed-interfaces for low speed printers for special applications. The projects were special applications oriented and highly customized, which were successfully consummated for the DEC special customers. The required reports were prepared and filed with Information Processing Center, DEC.

IBM :

Summer 1974. Worked for IBM Thomas J. Watson Research Center, Yorktown Heights, New York, with Dr. Leo Esaki on System-Automation for Superlattice-fabrication of GaAs–AlAs to be employed in Ultrahigh speed IBM multi-instruction parallel processors. The System-automation team was technically advised at controlling the thickness of the Superlattice structure within a micron using the most innovative simulation techniques and shutter arrangements using IBM 1130 as a main control terminal for the mass-spectrometer used in the process.

Digital Equipment Corporation :

Summer 1973. Worked for DEC, Maynard, Massachusetts and technically advised the PDP-8 group in modifying the timer circuit for the PDP-8 processor in order to increase its efficiency. The timer was successfully modified reducing the total cycle time to 3.2 sec enhancing the efficiency by 60% which was later incorporated in PDP-8A and a report was submitted to the DEC.

MIT/Lincoln Lab :

Summers 1972-71. Worked for Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), Lincoln Laboratory, Lexington, Massachusetts on fabrication and testing of laboratory models of advanced state of the art "GaAs Millimeter Wave Diodes" to be integrated in low noise defense communication satellites. The 55 GHz Schottky barrier diodes were successfully developed and thoroughly tested for the first time in the world, which were used in the defense communication satellite launched in July 1975. The paper and report entitled "Optimization of the Performance of a Millimeter Wave Mixer Using GaAs Diodes" was prepared as listed under publications.
